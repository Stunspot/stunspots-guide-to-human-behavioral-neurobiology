# A. Field Ontology, Levels of Analysis, and Causal Boundaries

## **Foundational Ontology: Cognition as a Scale-Free Regulatory Ecology**

The architectural framework of human behavior cannot be sufficiently explained by isolating neurobiology, relying on folk psychology, or treating social interactions as mere decoration for neural activity. Instead, cognition must be conceptualized as a distributed, scale-free regulatory ecology. This field ontology is anchored in the Free Energy Principle (FEP) and its behavioral implementation, active inference, which formalize how adaptive self-organizing systems maintain structural, operational, and phenotypic coherence against entropic decay.1 Living organisms do not passively process environmental inputs; they exist as recursive, prediction-driven loops where the boundary between the internal architecture of the system and the external world is defined statistically rather than purely anatomically.4

Under this paradigm, the primary imperative of any self-organizing entity is to minimize variational free energy, an information-theoretic bound on surprise or the negative log-evidence of the system’s generative model.4 This foundational imperative is expressed through the coherence principle:

This principle describes how locally bounded systems maintain homeostatic and phenotypic coherence within a wider environment under conditions of persistent uncertainty.2 Active inference extends this formulation by demonstrating that organisms minimize prediction error through two complementary pathways: perceptual inference, which updates internal beliefs to align with sampled sensory data, and active inference proper, which alters the environment through action to fulfill internal predictions.3 Action and perception are therefore fundamentally inseparable; action drives perception by selecting the sensory streams that confirm the generative model's primary assumptions.3

This regulatory ecology operates across nested spatial and temporal scales, ranging from intracellular molecular cascades to macro-scale socio-cultural institutions.8 To trace the evolutionary lineage of this architecture, the computational framework can be observed in its simplest form within bacterial taxis.10 This molecular blueprint demonstrates that even single-celled organisms utilize proximal internal states indexed by basic homeostatic requirements to navigate environmental resource gradients, providing the foundational template for embodiment.10

As biological systems scale in complexity, the brain's gray matter becomes partitioned into specialized cognitive and physiological homeostatic domains.10 These distinct domains are integrated dynamically through the twin mechanisms of affect and self-efficacy, utilizing the structural principle of redundancy to preserve regulatory stability across changing environments.10

To optimize these processing loops, the brain implements predictive coding as a primary signal compression strategy, functioning analogously to signal processing formats such as MP3 encoding or electronic toys like the Speak & Spell.4 Rather than continuously transmitting comprehensive sensory streams, the expected features of an input signal are suppressed top-down, ensuring that only unexpected features—the prediction errors—are conveyed up the processing hierarchy.4

However, given the vast number of internal and external variables an organism must navigate, inference and learning can easily become computationally intractable.4 To prevent systemic collapse, these complex computations must be continuously tested, calibrated, and modeled *in silico* or scaffolded through external configurations to ensure survival.4

## **Mathematical Individuation and the Mechanics of Statistical Boundaries**

The formal individuation of an adaptive system within this scale-free hierarchy is achieved through the construct of the Markov blanket.5 A Markov blanket establishes a statistical partition between the internal states of a system (mu) and its external environment (eta).5 This boundary is not a rigid physical barrier but a conditional independence framework composed of a bipartition into sensory states (s) and active states (a).5 Internal states can influence external states only via active states, and external states can influence internal states only via sensory states.5 This shielding mechanism ensures that internal states remain insulated from direct external perturbations, allowing the system to maintain its structural integrity while participating in continuous bidirectional exchanges with its niche.5

This structural partitioning reveals that the boundaries of cognition are flexible and context-dependent.5 Depending on the specific focus of inquiry, the relevant Markov blanket can sit at the level of a single neuron, an entire organism's skin membrane, or an extended socio-technical system.5 Cognition is therefore defined by the variational mechanics that straddle and integrate across these boundaries rather than by any single internal locus.5

When individual agents interact repeatedly within an ecology, their active and sensory states become coupled, giving rise to emergent macro-scale blankets.9 This multiscale integration is compatible with distributed cognition frameworks, demonstrating that what is traditionally categorized as "mind" extends beyond the skull into constitutive engagements with artifacts and social institutions.11

## **Neuroenergetics, Predictive Interoception, and Allostatic Architecture**

At the organismic level, the primary task of the generative model is the regulation of physiological stability, a challenge that shifts the focus from classical homeostasis to predictive allostasis.18 While homeostasis describes reactive negative feedback loops designed to keep internal parameters clamped at rigid, localized setpoints, allostasis represents feedforward control where the brain anticipates future physiological demands and adapts parameters preemptively to match expected environmental challenges.10

Proof of this predictive regulation is visible in daily physiological rhythms, such as the natural elevation of blood pressure in the morning before standing up, or the downregulation of heart rates during sleep to match reduced metabolic demands.10 These variations, rather than signifying error or failure, are explicitly designed to minimize regulatory mismatch and optimize energy expenditure across fluctuating environments.19

This predictive orchestration is mediated by the distributed allostatic-interoceptive brain network, which controls the body's immune, endocrine, and autonomic systems.14 The structural baseline of this network is detailed by the Embodied Predictive Interoception Coding (EPIC) model, which integrates cortical laminar architecture with the principles of active inference.7 Less-differentiated, agranular and dysgranular visceromotor cortices within limbic regions—specifically the anterior insula and cingulate cortices—function as the origin of top-down interoceptive predictions.7

These predictions cascade down to highly differentiated granular sensory cortices, such as the primary interoceptive cortex in the dorsal mid-to-posterior insula, where they are compared against ascending visceral sensations.7 The structural tension between these layers computes interoceptive prediction errors, which are either sent back up the cortical hierarchy to modify the generative model or resolved by driving autonomic effectors to alter the internal state of the body.7

This architectural flow relies on precision weighting, which scales the influence of prediction errors based on their estimated reliability or inverse variance.4 Precision functions as an associative learning rate or sensory volume control; highly reliable prediction errors demand immediate belief updating, whereas low-precision signals are discounted as random environmental noise.4 The global management of this predictive loop is sustained by an integrated network architecture combining the default mode network (DMN) and the salience network (SN) through highly connected rich-club hubs, such as the midcingulate cortex and the anterior insula.15 Limbic regions within the salience network indirectly modulate the precision of incoming inputs by acting upon the reticular nucleus encircling the thalamus, thereby filtering the sensory information allowed to reach the cortex via thalamocortical pathways.15

Crucially, the brain exhibits continuous structural and neurochemical plasticity across these regions in response to allostatic demands.21 While short-term challenges promote adaptive learning and growth, chronic activation of these pathways causes significant structural and molecular wear-and-tear.21 Prolonged anxiety and constant anticipation of threat drive the continuous output of allostatic biomediators, altering baseline physiological states.18

Over time, this chronic strain results in epigenetic modifications, such as the permanent thickening of blood vessel walls to sustain elevated cardiac output and the down-regulation of stress hormone receptors.19 A key feature of disorders of stress adaptation is the loss of this structural resilience; however, because this plasticity is bidirectional, the reversibility of these maladaptive neural modifications underpins long-term treatment efficacy and recovery.22

This physiological progression highlights that the internal systems of the body cannot be evaluated in isolation from external behaviors.22 Lifestyle behaviors—including diet, sleep architecture, physical exercise, and alcohol or nicotine consumption—are not merely personal choices but are dynamic components of the organism's allostatic loop, directly altering the metabolic resources available to maintain neural and somatic resilience.22

## **The Metabolic Economy of Social Baselines and Eusocial Scale-Up**

The human brain is an exceptionally expensive metabolic organ, consuming approximately 20% of the body’s total energy expenditure at rest.24 Because it operates almost exclusively on glucose oxidized at near-maximum rates, it possesses virtually no intrinsic metabolic reserve, meaning that managing the neural energy budget is a matter of immediate survival.24 Social Baseline Theory (SBT) addresses this constraint by demonstrating that the human brain is phylogenetically designed to assume immediate proximity to a familiar, predictable social network as its default ecological state.25 Social integration functions as a primary strategy for energy conservation through two distinct mechanics: risk distribution and load sharing.24

Risk distribution reduces the individual metabolic cost of environmental vigilance.24 A coupled social group provides multiple eyes and ears to monitor for environmental dangers, allowing individual members to down-regulate costly prefrontal vigilance circuits and conserve neural resources.24 Load sharing involves the direct outsourcing of physical and cognitive tasks—such as resource gathering, collective problem solving, and mutual emotion regulation—to trusted network members.25 Secure interpersonal bonds, supported by oxytocinergic, dopaminergic, and endogenous opioid signaling, allow individual brains to treat the capacities of social partners as an extension of their own regulatory model.25

Conversely, relative social isolation or perceived loneliness constitutes a direct violation of this baseline evolutionary assumption.25 When social support is absent or perceived as unreliable, the brain is forced to repatriate its outsourced regulatory functions, operating at a significantly higher individual metabolic expense.24 This metabolic shift triggers a cascade of physiological adaptations:

This cascade highlights that behaviors often labeled as failures of self-control, such as the elevated consumption of sugar-containing beverages by lonely individuals, are actually direct systemic compensations driven by an energy-depleted brain attempting to restore its depleted glucose reserves.24 Over a trans-temporal timeline, this chronic allostatic strain damages peripheral tissues, leading to systemic insulin resistance, cardiovascular disease, and a elevated risk for type 2 diabetes.18 What appears at a micro-level as a localized metabolic disorder is revealed at a macro-level to be a failure of social co-regulation.

This multiscale Bayesian framework maps precisely onto collective biological systems, demonstrating that social co-regulation is a scale-free property of life.9 For example, the collective behavior of an ant colony can be modeled using a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) under active inference.8 Eusocial insect colonies operate as a unified unit of function shaped by natural selection, reflecting changes to a shared, multi-level inferential scheme.9

Through stigmergic regulation, individual foragers utilize a single trail pheromone molecule to cache behavioral information directly into the environment.9 Even though each individual nestmate has highly limited access to incoming sensory information, the collective group successfully solves complex foraging problems by reading and modifying these environmental pheromone trails.9 Information-seeking and goal-seeking values are outsourced to the niche, showing how group-level optimization emerges naturally from coupled active inference agents.9

## **Cultural Niche Construction, Material Culture, and the Dialectic of Entropy**

To stabilize these metabolic demands over long timescales, human communities actively engineer their environments through cultural niche construction, a process where the neural niche and the environmental niche co-evolve.12 This framework rejects a naive model of the brain as an unconstrained general learning device.28 Instead, as articulated by Stanislas Dehaene’s concept of neuronal recycling, cultural practices and cognitive tools are constrained by evolutionarily pre-existing neural precursors, transforming the cognitive system through deep historical processes of enculturation.28

This material entanglement is reflected in cognitive archaeology, which demonstrates that the human mind and its functions are the result of engagements with material culture rather than simply its cause.11 Artifact records serve as a "fossil trail" not just of the products of past thought (the grist), but of parts of the thinking machinery itself (the mill).11 For example, a blind man's cane does not merely provide data to the hand; it constitutively extends the man's perceptual experience through the reachability affordances it physically creates, demonstrating that tools are actively integrated into the organism's active inference loops.11

Human communities scale this material integration by establishing Regimes of Expectations (ROEs), which are shared, normative frameworks that prescribe typical and admitted forms of social behavior, preferences, and values within a given community.17 ROEs organize the social world to make it highly predictable for individual agents, functioning via three core mechanisms:

* **Natural Pedagogy:** Individual agents acquire ROEs during ontogeny through implicit learning biases, automatically copying, imitating, and inferring the expectations of authoritative, prestigious, or trusted in-group figures.17 This mechanism shifts policy selection from dangerous individual trial-and-error to structured, group-vetted assimilation.17  
* **Epistemic Authority:** Environmental and social cues signaling prestige act as markers of reliability, directing the agent’s attentional resources toward high-value normative behaviors and minimizing the computational energy spent on evaluating alternative paths.17  
* **Deontic Cues and Value:** Human environments are intentionally structured with deontic cues—physical or material markers that denote explicit social rules (e.g., traffic signals or architectural layouts).17 These cues possess deontic value, a computational property that directly shapes policy selection by dominating individual pragmatic or hedonistic utilities.17 This configuration allows the epistemic and pragmatic values of behavior to be cached directly into the material environment, which effectively "learns" and preserves the behavioral history of its denizens across transgenerational timescales.17

Crucially, this regulatory ecology operates through a complex dialectic of entropy.12 While standard cultural practices are negentropic—meaning they seek to reduce entropy and maximize predictability across scales—human systems also willfully engage in entropic cultural practices.12 This deliberate engagement with uncertainty is foundational to human play, creative activities, and cultural entertainment.12 Humans regularly navigate unexpected musical patterns, laugh at unpredictable comedy punchlines, participate in games involving unpredictable dice throws or card draws, and engage in the open-ended, exploratory walking practices of the psychogeographic *dérive* (drift).12

This entropic engagement is explained through the active inference model of aesthetic experience, which conceptualizes art and play as an epistemic arc composed of three parts: curiosity, epistemic action, and the "aha" experience.29 Artworks and playful behaviors intentionally construct, sustain, and manipulate uncertainty rather than rushing to immediate closure.29

By navigating these controlled doses of unpredictability, the generative model refines its learning capacity, preventing the system from becoming overfitted to a static environment.12 This dialectic defuses overconfident, reductionist models by demonstrating that play, openness, and lingering uncertainty are core requirements of adaptive cognitive design.12

## **Epistemic Guardrails and Cross-Level Causal Grammar**

To govern the interpretation of human behavior within this interpretive constitution, explicit cross-level reasoning rules must be enforced to prevent reductionist, moralizing, or overconfident explanations. When analyzing behavioral, somatic, or psychological dysfunctions, it is an epistemic error to locate the "lesion" exclusively within a single sub-system or localized neural molecule.30 Complex phenotypic states represent multi-scale misfits across the brain-body-niche continuum, driven by suboptimal parameter settings within the generative model.6

To ensure systemic rigor, the following causal grammar must be maintained:

1. **Causal Claims Must Specify Statistical Insulation:** No macro-scale cultural force or social norm can directly alter internal neural states without explicitly passing through and updating the sensory and active states of the relevant Markov blankets.5 All top-down formatting must be accounted for through specific attentional, precision, or visceromotor routing mechanisms.17  
2. **Pathology is an Inversion of Predictive Efficiency:** Biological and psychological disorders should be framed as a systemic loss of predictive flexibility—where the generative model fails to maintain allostatic variability and becomes locked in metabolically inefficient configurations.20  
3. **Behavior is Non-Moralistic Optimization:** Maladaptive behaviors must be interpreted as the generative model's optimal, resource-constrained solution to aberrant precision weighting or depleted metabolic baselines, rather than as willful moral failures.6  
4. **Developmental Timing Formats the Baseline Trajectory:** The impact of environmental stressors is heavily dependent on developmental stage and timing.23 Early childhood represents a period of extreme plasticity where environmental risks and parenting behaviors (such as maternal grooming patterns) directly format DNA methylation and neural development.23 These early experiences establish the foundational settings of the generative model, meaning that adult psychopathology often reflects structural adaptations optimized for early adversity but mismatched with current contexts.23

## **Systematic Guidelines for Strategic Application**

To implement Report A as the interpretive constitution for all subsequent research and design applications within the knowledge-base canon, human systems must be evaluated through three integrated dimensions:

* **The Metabolic Ledger:** Every behavioral policy carries an energetic cost that must be accounted for.24 Before designing interventions to alter behavior or modify an organization's regime of expectations, designers must map the social, physical, and cognitive resources currently buffering the agent's prefrontal energy consumption.17 If an intervention removes a social support network, a material tool, or an environmental routine without providing an alternative method for load sharing, the system will naturally slide toward compensatory, high-entropy actions to protect its remaining metabolic reserves.12  
* **The Predictive Topography:** Interventions must target the appropriate layer of the prediction-error hierarchy.7 If an organizational or clinical system exhibits chronic allostatic load, modifying macro-scale rules will remain ineffective if the agents' low-level visceromotor networks are locked in central sensitization.7 True systemic change requires alignment across scales, ensuring that ascending sensory predictability is actively matched by top-down deontic structuring.14  
* **Niche Legibility and Play:** The physical and informational environments engineered by institutions must present clear, low-friction deontic cues that allow agents to effortlessly cache their processing costs.17 High-stress, ambiguous, or rapidly shifting environments distort precision weighting, trigger false inferences, and force agents back onto highly inefficient, localized trial-and-error behaviors.4 However, designer environments must also provide structured spaces for entropic play and aesthetic exploration, allowing generative models to safely test uncertainty and maintain systemic resilience.12 Building a sustainable cognitive ecology requires designing settings that actively stabilize the metabolic and predictive demands of the organisms living within them.12
