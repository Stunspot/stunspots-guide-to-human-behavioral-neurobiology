## **Stress as Adaptive Mobilization**

The stress response is a highly conserved, costly mobilization policy deployed to reallocate metabolic resources in anticipation of, or in response to, environmental demands.4 Within a systems-neurobiology framework, a strict distinction must be maintained between the *stressor*—the objective physical, metabolic, or psychological disruption to homeostasis—and the *stress response*, which is the coordinated physiological cascade designed to meet that challenge.4 The evolutionary utility of this response is entirely dependent upon its context, including its controllability, predictability, duration, intensity, and the availability of clear recovery access.4

When a challenge is registered, the central nervous system activates a dual-axis response comprising the rapid sympathetic-adrenal-medullary (SAM) axis and the slower, sustained hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.4 The SAM axis operates on a millisecond-to-second timescale, driven by preganglionic sympathetic neurons that trigger the release of epinephrine and norepinephrine from the adrenal medulla and terminal varicosities.5 This acute catecholaminergic surge alters cardiovascular dynamics, expands respiratory capacity, upregulates systemic glucose availability, and sharpens central sensory gating to optimize physical readiness.5 Concurrently, it induces a transient state of stress-induced analgesia, raising pain thresholds to ensure that nociceptive inputs do not interrupt defensive actions, while redistributing circulating immune cells out from vascular reservoirs and into peripheral tissues where immediate wounding or pathogen infiltration is anticipated.5

On a minutes-to-hours timescale, the HPA axis is recruited to sustain this energetic mobilization and prevent the initial physiological reaction from overshooting and causing auto-immunological damage.4 Cortisol acts as an adaptive mobilization signal that alters metabolic substrates by promoting gluconeogenesis, inhibiting peripheral insulin sensitivity, and liberating free fatty acids to fuel continuous high-demand neural and muscular performance.4 Simultaneously, elevated glucocorticoid signaling downregulates non-essential, high-cost metabolic investments, transiently suppressing reproductive functions, digestive processes, and long-term tissue growth.4 Stress is not an inherent disorder; it is a costly mobilization policy whose usefulness depends on duration, controllability, predictability, recovery access, and ecological fit.6

## **HPA-Axis Dynamics and Termination**

The HPA axis operates through a highly regulated neuroendocrine cascade that relies on complex feedforward drives and precise negative feedback loops to maintain physiological stability.4 The sequence begins in the parvocellular neurons of the hypothalamic paraventricular nucleus (PVN), which synthesize and secrete corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) and arginine vasopressin (AVP) into the hypophyseal portal system in response to neural inputs from limbic and brainstem networks.2 These neuropeptides bind to specific receptors on the cortiotroph cells of the anterior guessing gland, driving the cleavage of pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC) and the subsequent secretion of adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into the systemic circulation.4 Upon reaching the adrenal cortex, ACTH binds to melanocortin type 2 receptors in the zona fasciculata, stimulating the immediate synthesis and release of glucocorticoids—primarily cortisol in humans.4

Systemic cortisol levels exhibit a pronounced circadian rhythm, characterized by a sharp cortisol awakening response (CAR) that peaks approximately 30 to 45 minutes post-awakening, followed by a steady decline throughout the day toward a circadian nadir in the early hours of the night.15 Superimposed upon this circadian curve is an ultradian rhythm marked by pulsatile secretory bursts; acute stressors that coincide with the rising phase of an endogenous ultradian pulse elicit significantly larger total glucocorticoid excursions than those applied during the falling phase.4

The regulation and termination of this cascade are governed by two distinct nuclear receptor families: high-affinity mineralocorticoid receptors (MR) and low-affinity glucocorticoid receptors (GR).15 MRs possess an affinity for cortisol roughly ten times greater than that of GRs, meaning they remain largely saturated under basal conditions and during the circadian nadir, where they exert a continuous, stabilizing inhibitory influence over basal HPA-axis drive, primarily via hippocampal networks.18 Conversely, GRs have a low affinity for cortisol and are recruited primarily during major circadian peaks or acute stress surges.18 The activation of GRs in the pituitary, PVN, and hippocampus initiates a robust transcriptomic and non-genomic negative feedback loop that suppresses further CRH and ACTH release, acting as the primary mechanism for allostatic termination.4 This intra-cellular signaling environment is further modulated by the prereceptor metabolic enzymes 11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1 (11-beta-HSD1), which regenerates active cortisol from inert cortisone inside target cells, and 11-beta-HSD2, which inactivates cortisol to protect sensitive tissues from steroid overload.18

When an organism is subjected to chronic, uncontrollable allostatic load, this precise feedback architecture degrades, transitioning the system into allostatic overload.6 Persistent hypercortisolemia induces a progressive downregulation and desensitization of central GRs, a pathological state known as glucocorticoid resistance.6 In this state, despite high circulating cortisol levels, the central nervous system fails to register the negative feedback signal, causing the HPA axis to remain uncoupled from homeostatic constraints and locked into a state of continuous, destructive resource mobilization.5 This chronic hypercortisolemia induces profound neural remodeling, characterized by dendritic atrophy and reduced neurogenesis in the hippocampus and medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), alongside dendritic hypertrophy in the basolateral amygdala (BLA)—a structural rearrangement that systematically impairs future safety learning and locks the organism into a permanent state of threat readiness.4 Alternatively, under specific conditions of chronic trauma or exhaustion, the system may exhibit a hypocortisol-like pattern characterized by a flattened diurnal slope and an exaggerated GR negative feedback sensitivity, restricting the organism’s capacity to mobilize energy when authentic environmental demands arise.6

This central termination process is regulated by specific receptor-region cross-talk involving the endocannabinoid system (ECS) during safety learning and fear extinction.5 Under acute stress, elevated glucocorticoids engage membrane-associated receptors on principal neurons within the BLA and hypothalamus, triggering the rapid, on-demand synthesis and release of the retrograde lipid messenger 2-arachidonoylglycerol (2-AG).5 This 2-AG travels retrogradely across the synaptic cleft to bind presynaptic cannabinoid type-1 (CB1) receptors localized predominantly on local GABAergic terminal varicosities.5 Activation of these CB1 receptors engages inhibitory Gi/o proteins, suppressing adenylyl cyclase activity, blocking voltage-gated calcium channels, and reducing the probability of vesicular GABA release.5 This rapid disinhibition allows principal amygdalar neurons to fire robustly, executing the immediate behavioral and autonomic maneuvers required to escape a threat.5

Concurrently, however, acute emotional stress drives a sharp reduction in the second primary endocannabinoid, anandamide (AEA), within the amygdala and hypothalamus.5 This drop is mediated by a CRH-dependent up-regulation of fatty acid amide hydrolase (FAAH), the primary catabolic enzyme responsible for AEA degradation.5 Because baseline AEA signaling via tonic CB1 activation normally maintains a continuous inhibitory constraint over CRH release in the PVN, the stress-induced collapse of AEA unloads this molecular brake, enabling the HPA axis to map its full endocrine surge.5

During subsequent safety learning and fear extinction, this system must reverse to achieve allostatic termination.5 When the environment signals that a threat has resolved, retrograde endocannabinoid transients engage amygdalar and hypothalamic CB1 receptors to actively restrict further hypothalamic CRH release and blunt sympathetic output.5 This retrograde signaling suppresses excessive glutamatergic drive onto the PVN, reinstating the inhibitory baseline and allowing circulating cortisol to successfully engage central GRs to terminate the stress response.5 If chronic stress persistently upregulates FAAH activity, AEA levels remain permanently depleted, inducing a severe downregulation of central CB1 receptors.5 This molecular lesion destroys the organism’s capacity for ECS-mediated allostatic termination, uncoupling the sympathetic nervous system from HPA-axis constraints, rendering the amygdala chronically hyperexcitable, and preventing the updating of safety memories.5

## **Autonomic State Dynamics**

The autonomic nervous system shapes the body’s readiness for engagement, vigilance, defense, conservation, digestion, repair, and sleep.7 Rather than operating as a binary toggle, the ANS balances sympathetic and parasympathetic inputs to prepare the whole body for specific motor and behavioral scripts.7 Sympathetic activation increases heart rate and blood pressure, shifts blood flow to skeletal muscles, increases respiration rate, dilates pupils, and elevates skin conductance via sweat gland activation while suppressing digestion.5 Parasympathetic regulation, primarily mediated by the vagus nerve, acts to slow the heart, lower blood pressure, stimulate digestion, and support immune and tissue repair.9

A critical proxy of central-peripheral integration is respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA)—the natural acceleration of heart rate during inhalation and deceleration during exhalation, which serves as an index of cardiac vagal drive.23 The vagus nerve acts as a dynamic "vagal brake" on the sinoatrial node, allowing rapid changes in heart rate without the immediate deployment of the sympathetic-adrenal system.7 This autonomic tone is supported by baroreflex sensitivity, which regulates blood pressure variations to ensure cardiovascular readiness.27

When evaluating these patterns, it is important to extract useful physiological distinctions rather than turning autonomic states into personality types, moral labels, or diagnostic identities.9 The orienting response, active mobilization (fight-or-flight), defensive freezing, and shutdown-like conservation represent distinct physiological profiles designed to navigate specific configurations of safety and hazard.7

## **Autonomic Flexibility Versus State-Lock**

Healthy regulation is not permanent calm. Healthy regulation is flexible state switching: mobilize when needed, recover when possible, sleep when protected, conserve when ill, engage when safe, and return to baseline without heroic effort.3 In a healthy organism, autonomic flexibility ensures that resource allocation matches the immediate demand before downshifting to baseline once the demand resolves.7

Systemic dysfunction emerges when this fluid switching architecture breaks down, causing the physiology to narrow into a rigid state-lock.1 State-lock patterns—including sympathetic dominance, hypervigilance lock, insomnia-hyperarousal lock, sickness-conservation lock, inflammatory malaise lock, freeze/shutdown conservation lock, fatigue-collapse lock, and compulsive threat-monitoring lock—represent configurations where a defensive policy remains active past its adaptive window, creating a structural barrier to recovery and generating allostatic debt.1

## **Sleep as State Repair Architecture**

Sleep is not a luxury recovery behavior; it is the daily state-transition system that reopens regulatory flexibility.3 Sleep regulation is driven by the interaction of the homeostatic sleep drive (Process S), which tracks the accumulation of extracellular adenosine as a byproduct of waking ATP utilization, and the circadian drive (Process C), generated by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) using light-dark cycles to gate sleep timing.2 Natural sleep shifts cyclically between Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM) and Rapid Eye Movement (REM) states.12

Stage N3 NREM, or slow-wave sleep (SWS), is characterized by high-amplitude slow oscillations (0.5-4 Hz) generated by synchronized cortical pyramidal cells alternating between silent down-states and active up-states.25 SWS supports metabolic restoration, triggers growth hormone secretion, lowers sympathetic drive, rebuilds astrocytic glycogen, and facilitates the consolidation of declarative memory.12 REM sleep provides a unique neurochemical environment for emotional memory recalibration, synaptic homeostasis, and nightmare-associated threat simulation within protected boundaries.12

During SWS, brain clearance is accelerated through the glymphatic system, where the suppression of noradrenergic tone expands the interstitial space fraction to lower resistance to cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) bulk flow, clearing neurotoxic solutes like beta-amyloid and hyperphosphorylated tau.28 However, human translation critiques note that the contribution of glymphatic flow versus simple diffusion remains difficult to measure non-invasively, and commonly used sedatives can create a "pseudo-sleep" that mimics cortical unconsciousness on an EEG while suppressing the noradrenergic oscillations required for clearance.31

In resolving the first unresolved question from Report E, the transition from acute sickness conservation to chronic low-grade neuroinflammation degrades SWS delta architecture through a specific neuroimmune pathway.1 During prolonged wakefulness or chronic sleep fragmentation, increased neural activity drives the accumulation of extracellular ATP in cortical and subcortical circuits.12 This extracellular ATP binds to low-affinity purinergic P2X7 receptors on microglia, activating the canonical Functional Nuclear Factor kappa-light-chain-enhancer of activated B cells (NF-kappaB) and Mitogen-activated protein kinase (MAPK) pathways.12 This cascade drives the assembly of the NLRP3 inflammasome, accelerating the maturation and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, specifically interleukin-1 beta (IL-1beta), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-alpha), and interleukin-6 (IL-6).12

While transient elevations of these cytokines track homeostatic sleep pressure, their chronic central accumulation modifies local neural membrane excitability and disrupts serotonergic (5-HT) neuromodulation.3 This persistent neuroinflammatory state downregulates the scaffold protein targeting to glycogen (PTG) in astrocytes, disrupting glycogen turnover and impairing the synchronization of cortical pyramidal networks.30 The result is a structural fragmentation of slow-wave oscillations, a loss of sleep spindles, and a degradation of the delta power spectrum, which collapses glymphatic clearance and traps the organism in a hyperarousal lock.1

This process is exacerbated by the third unresolved question from Report E: the biophysical cascade linking chronic high-frequency noradrenergic volume transmission to locus coeruleus vulnerability and tau phosphorylation risk.14 Noradrenergic neurons in the locus coeruleus (LC) project throughout the brain, releasing norepinephrine (NE) via volume transmission to activate extrasynaptic adrenergic GPCRs over extended timescales.16 Under chronic stress, the LC is locked into a pattern of high tonic firing.14 This continuous noradrenergic volume transmission acts on gray matter astrocytes to drive rapid, continuous glycogenolysis.11

Because astrocytic glycogen serves as the short-term energy buffer supporting potassium (K+) clearance and glutamate uptake via the glutamate-glutamine cycle, chronic LC drive causes absolute astrocytic glycogen depletion.11 This metabolic exhaustion crashes local potassium and glutamate clearance, leading to local microvascular leakage and blood-brain barrier permeability alterations.1 The resulting local metabolic stress activates glycogen synthase kinase-3 beta (GSK-3beta), which drives the hyperphosphorylation of native tau into insoluble pretangle tau (pTau), establishing Braak Stage 0 pathology in the LC core.30 This structural damage alters mitochondrial efficiency and increases calcium channel expression, inducing a state of local energetic stress where surviving LC neurons become hyperactive to compensate, locking the organism into a feedforward cycle of high tonic NE volume transmission that prevents SWS downshifts and damages sleep-linked restoration.14

## **Circadian Regulation and Temporal Gating**

The temporal coordination of organism-level state dynamics is managed by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), which translates light-dark entrainment via the retinohypothalamic tract into systemic synchronization.2 The SCN gates the rhythmicity of cortisol mobilization, melatonin release, core body temperature oscillations, feeding timing, glucose tolerance, and immune cell redistribution.2

Because these systems are in a state of continuous temporal flux, a biomarker without circadian context is often a fortune cookie with decimals. Cortisol, HRV, core temperature, glucose, and circulating cytokines can only be interpreted when referenced against the organism's endogenous chronotype and current circadian phase.2 Circadian misalignment—driven by shift work, irregular schedules, or social jet lag—acts as a severe ecological constraint, forcing the active inference engine to select policies when the underlying visceral biology is unaligned, elevating the metabolic control costs of daily functions and driving chronic allostatic load.1

## **Immunity as Behavioral State Controller**

The immune system acts as a central behavioral state controller, modulating motivation, energy expenditure, and policy selection during illness or injury.3 When peripheral immune cells detect pathogens or tissue damage, they release pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1beta, IL-6, TNF-alpha) that signal the central nervous system through fast vagal afferents projecting to the nucleus of the solitary tract, and slower humoral pathways involving endothelial prostaglandin (PGE2) synthesis.3 This neuroimmune communication drives *sickness behavior*—an active immune-motivational conservation policy that reallocates energy away from external behavior and toward host defense, tissue repair, fever, pain sensitivity, and recovery.3 This profile involves lethargy, social withdrawal, hyperalgesia, anorexia, and an altered sleep architecture optimized to lower the metabolic costs of movement and redirect resources toward the immune program.3

In resolving the second unresolved question from Report E, metabolic and immune signals reshape recovery priorities, appetite, and reward processing through explicit structural interactions.13 The dorsomedial hypothalamus (DMH) integrates inputs from the arcuate nucleus, which monitors peripheral concentrations of leptin, ghrelin, and insulin to track somatic energy reserves.2 The DMH sends projections to the sleep-regulating ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO), the neuroendocrine paraventricular nucleus (PVN), and the wake-promoting lateral hypothalamus (LH).2

During chronic stress or infection, elevated peripheral cytokines (IL-6) and altered cortisol drive alter this homeostatic network.13 These somatic signals are integrated by the primary interoceptive cortex in the posterior and mid-insula, suppressing dopamine transporter functionality and blunting reward responsiveness (RR) within the ventral striatum, medial orbitofrontal cortex, and putamen.13

This reward hyposensitivity increases the computed control cost of effort, biasing policy selection toward approach avoidance and motivational flattening.13 In the decreased appetite subgroup, elevated cortisol correlates with a suppressed ventral striatal response to food cues, driving systemic anorexia.13 In the increased appetite subgroup, immunometabolic dysregulation characterized by insulin resistance and hyperleptinemia drives a compensatory hyper-responsiveness to simple food cues within the insula, forcing short-horizon compulsive feeding policies to rapidly protect against energetic collapse.13

Concurrently, during catabolic tissue breakdown, an alternative gut-to-brain signaling pathway monitors the accumulation of systemic ammonia—a byproduct of protein deamination.40 This metabolic signal drives a strong, specific aversion to protein-rich food, acting as a protective mechanism that limits nitrogenous waste production while the active inference engine redirects recovery priorities toward baseline tissue stabilization.40

## **Stress–Sleep–Immune Coupling**

The physiological states of stress mobilization, sleep architecture, and immune regulation form a reciprocal, closed-loop feedback network where changes in one domain alter the entire system.1

### **The Adaptive Loop**

The adaptive loop operates across clean, time-delimited phases where resource expenditure is balanced by protected recovery windows 7:

Predictable Demand -> Acute Mobilization -> Successful Action -> Parasympathetic Recovery -> Sleep Restoration -> Immune Resolution -> Flexible Baseline

In this configuration, a predictable environmental demand is met with a controlled catecholaminergic and glucocorticoid surge, temporarily shifting the policy economy to prioritize immediate execution.4 Once the task is complete, the brain registers exteroceptive safety cues, triggering endocannabinoid-mediated allostatic termination.5 The system transitions to parasympathetic regulation, lowering cardiovascular load and enabling SWS.7 During SWS, noradrenergic suppression drives glymphatic waste clearance, while nocturnal cytokines coordinate memory consolidation and tissue repair, returning the organism to a flexible baseline.3

### **The Maladaptive Chronic Loop**

When stressors are unresolved, unpredictable, or uncontrollable, this coupling transitions into a self-reinforcing, destructive loop 1:

Unresolved Demand -> Sympathetic/HPA Activation -> Sleep Fragmentation -> Immune Dysregulation -> Inflammatory Signaling -> Fatigue / Reward Blunting -> Reduced Recovery Behavior -> Further Mobilization

Here, a failure of allostatic termination drives continuous sympathetic and HPA output, causing central GR downregulation and endocannabinoid system depletion.5 This hyperarousal fragments sleep architecture, depleting SWS delta power and truncating REM emotional processing.12 The loss of SWS impairs glymphatic clearance, causing extracellular ATP and toxic proteins to accumulate within the parenchymal matrix.12 This accumulation serves as a meta-trigger that activates microglial P2X7 pathways, sustaining chronic low-grade neuroinflammation.1 These central cytokines act on the insula and mesolimbic reward circuits to blunt reward responsiveness and drive severe fatigue, reducing the organism's capacity to engage in recovery behaviors.13 Because the active inference engine interprets this internal visceral distress as an ongoing threat, it downregulates safety priors and drives further mobilization, locking the organism into an accelerating loop of allostatic debt.5

## **Recovery Windows and Ecological Permission**

Recovery is a physiological state transition requiring permissive conditions, not a moral virtue or wellness aesthetic.7 The downshift into parasympathetic accessibility and sleep-linked restoration cannot be prescriptively commanded; it requires that the organism's environment satisfy objective boundary conditions.1 These conditions include physical safety, social safety, low sensory load, predictability, circadian alignment, trusted co-regulation, agency, and an absence of acute metabolic or immune volatility.1

Recovery access is an ecological property, not merely an individual choice.7 Hostile workplaces, surveillance systems, shift schedules, digital interruption, financial scarcity, caregiving burdens, and social instability structurally block recovery by sending high-precision threat signals to the active inference engine.1 These persistent external demands make it impossible for the central nervous system to disengage vigilance, regardless of an individual's personal knowledge or intentions.7

## **Acute Versus Chronic Dynamics**

Time-collapse creates false explanations. A transient mobilization signal, a chronic baseline shift, a developmental calibration, and a disease-state dysregulation are not interchangeable.6 Acute sympathetic or HPA axis activation represents a highly adaptive, resource-reallocating adjustment to temporary environmental demands.4 Chronic activation, conversely, drives allostatic overload, transforming these preparatory adjustments into structural damage characterized by receptor downregulation, tissue atrophy, sleep architectural collapse, and persistent low-grade neuroinflammation.1 Conflating these timescales obscures the underlying regulatory mechanisms and leads to flawed diagnostic models.15

## **State-Dependent Cognition and Behavior**

The organism's regulatory state alters the computational parameters of cognition and behavior.7 Attention, working memory, impulse control, planning horizons, social interpretations, and reward responsiveness are not static traits; they are dynamically adjusted variables optimized to match the policy economy of the active state.13

Under a threat or hypervigilance lock, the active inference engine shifts its parameters to prioritize immediate survival.5 Attention is captured by high-salience threat cues, narrowing working memory and biasing policy selection toward automated habits and rapid reward-discounting.5 Ambiguity tolerance drops, causing neutral social signals to be categorized as hostile.5 Conversely, under an immune response or chronic inflammatory malaise, central cytokines blunt mesolimbic dopamine to inflate the predicted control costs of physical or social effort.13 This shift biases policy selection toward conservation, approach avoidance, and social withdrawal.3 Behavior reflects state-dependent policy selection under constraint, rather than isolated pathology or moral failure.7

## **Social and Environmental Regulation of State**

States are not generated solely inside the body; they are negotiated at organism-environment interfaces.7 The active inference engine continuously monitors external physical, social, and technological structures to scaffold its internal regulatory burden.7

To address the fourth unresolved question from Report E, the active inference engine represents the bioenergetic value of social and digital scaffolding through a competitive precision-weighting model. The brain calculates the free-energy cost of internal predictive control versus the cost of outsourcing processing to the environment.1 Trusted co-regulation functions as a high-value external metabolic scaffold; the exchange of facial, postural, and vocal safety cues allows the brain to lower the precision weighting on its internal threat-monitoring networks.7 This outsourcing drops the metabolic control cost of state maintenance, enabling the central vagal brake to engage and facilitating a transition into parasympathetic recovery.7

Conversely, when an organism encounters social isolation, relational volatility, or institutional surveillance, the environment signals structural uncertainty.6 The active inference engine calculates that external scaffolds are unreliable or dangerous, making it computationally unsafe to outsource threat monitoring.7 It must increase the precision weighting on its internal, self-reliant hypervigilance networks, accepting a high metabolic control cost to sustain continuous threat readiness.7

Similarly, digital interfaces designed with high-salience interruption schedules generate continuous prediction errors that fragment the attentional timeline.1 This interaction drains astrocytic energy reserves and signals to the active inference engine that it is operating in a volatile environment, preventing the downshift to a recovery configuration and reinforcing a state of chronic allostatic debt.7

## **Measurement and Proxy Risk**

Quantifying an organism's regulatory state requires a rigorous psychophysiological critique of biological markers. Every metric captured by consumer wearables or clinical assays is a proxy—an indirect, context-sensitive reflection of multi-system dynamics.8 Treating a proxy as an absolute measure of a psychological or emotional state introduces significant diagnostic errors.9

HRV is not a moral score or resilience essence; it is an approximate, context-sensitive proxy of cardiac autonomic regulation that is easily confounded by respiration rate, posture, and baseline health history.9 Cortisol is not “stress truth”; it is an adaptive metabolic mobilization signal whose values have no clinical meaning unless evaluated as part of a multi-point diurnal curve anchored to waking times.4 Circulating cytokines are not a depression diagnosis; they track systemic inflammatory tone and innate immune deployment, and cannot be assumed to directly mirror localized neuroinflammatory environments.1 Wearable sleep tracking stages are not clinical polysomnography; they rely on motion and pulse metrics to generate movement-based inferences that can induce orthosomnia—anxiety driven by wearable device sleep scores.8 Sympathetic arousal is not always fear, vagal tone is not virtue, shutdown behavior is not laziness, and fatigue is not a single mechanism.7

## **Polyvagal and Autonomic Theory Audit**

A balanced audit of Polyvagal Theory and adjacent autonomic frameworks is necessary to separate established physiology from popular overextensions.23 Polyvagal Theory usefully emphasizes the importance of the autonomic nervous system in shaping behavioral states, emotional experience, and social connection.7 It highlights the role of myelinated vagal pathways originating in the nucleus ambiguus (NAmb) in providing rapid cardiac regulation via a dynamic "vagal brake," and correctly links facial and vocal coordination to brainstem autonomic nuclei.7 These elements are well-supported by psychophysiological research and offer a helpful heuristic for clinicians and patients conceptualizing stress responses.7

However, multiple core evolutionary and neuroanatomical claims of the theory are widely criticized by comparative neurobiologists for being at odds with known science.23 Neuroanatomists point out that it is incorrect to claim that reptiles and non-mammalian vertebrates lack myelinated cardiac vagal efferents or do not exhibit complex social behaviors.9 Numerous comparative studies have shown that the cardiac vagus nerve in fishes, amphibians, reptiles, and birds includes myelinated axons capable of inducing near-instantaneous changes in heart rate, and respiration-related heart-rate variability is present across all vertebrate groups, serving to optimize cardioventilatory gas exchange rather than as an emergent mammalian adaptation for social regulation.9

Furthermore, popular trauma discourse frequently overextends the evidence by claiming that the dorsal motor nucleus of the vagus (DMNX) serves as an evolutionarily primitive defensive area that drives massive, lethal bradycardia, physical freezing, and psychological dissociation during emotional threat.9 Physiological research demonstrates that under normal conditions, the DMNX predominantly influences gut function, with a minimal effect on mammalian heart rate.9 Classical emotional fainting (vasovagal syncope) is driven by a transient withdrawal of sympathetic vascular tone combined with NAmb-mediated cardiodeceleration, not by a DMNX-driven reptilian collapse.9 Defensive freezing is a high-energy, co-activated state involving simultaneous sympathetic mobilization and NAmb vagal braking, rather than a low-energy dorsal shutdown.7 Autonomic states should not be treated as fixed identities, personality traits, or direct clinical diagnoses.9

## **Clinical Boundary and Ethical Guardrails**

This report is an explanatory systems-neurobiology manual and does not provide clinical guidance or medical advice.13 It does not diagnose PTSD, major depressive disorder, generalized anxiety, insomnia, dysautonomia, chronic fatigue syndrome, autoimmune disease, sleep apnea, long COVID, or burnout.6 It contains no diagnostic protocols, treatment recommendations, or lifestyle prescriptions.10 It does not prescribe sleep protocols, dietary interventions, exercise regimens, supplements, breathwork, vagus nerve stimulation, cold exposure, or therapeutic modalities.10 Visceral biomarkers are context-sensitive proxies and cannot be used to infer specific psychological states.9 This framework rejects the moralization of fatigue, sleep debt, or illness behaviors, and recognizes that structural environmental constraints cannot be resolved through individual nervous-system optimization alone.1
